# Knowledge Gaps: Transfer Pricing Quiz #6

*Gaps identified during Socratic dialogue sessions.*

## Accept/Reject Internal Transfer Decision (Analyze)
**Gap:** Added absorption cost to the accept/reject rule; needed the sunk cost connection to see why fixed costs are irrelevant to the decision.
**Teaching:** Accept if offer ≥ min TP (= variable cost + opportunity cost). Fixed costs are sunk — paid whether or not you accept — so they don't change the decision. The rule is purely about what is actually sacrificed.
**Verified:** yes

## Capacity Impact on Transfer Price (Analyze)
**Gap:** In the mixed case (some idle capacity, order exceeds it) student wrote TP = (demand − idle) × (market − variable) + idle × variable — missing the variable cost on the displaced-external portion. Caught the error via extreme-case sanity check (idle = 0).
**Teaching:** In mixed cases, split the order in two buckets. Units displacing external sales: charge full market price (which already includes variable cost). Units filling idle capacity: charge only variable cost. Total Min TP = (Demand − Idle Capacity) × Market Price + Idle Capacity × Variable Cost.
**Verified:** yes

## Minimum Transfer Price at Full Capacity (Apply)
**Gap:** Initially used full unit cost (variable + fixed) in the opportunity cost calculation; reasoned about manager controllability rather than sunk-cost logic when asked why only variable cost.
**Teaching:** Opportunity cost = market price − variable cost (not full unit cost). Fixed costs are sunk — they are paid whether the unit transfers internally or sells externally, so they don't vary with the choice and don't belong in opportunity cost. Min TP = Variable Cost + (Market Price − Variable Cost) = Market Price at full capacity.
**Verified:** yes

## Market-Based Transfer Pricing (Understand)
**Gap:** Did not know the definition of "perfectly competitive market" — the foundational term needed to reason about why market price is the ideal benchmark.
**Teaching:** A perfectly competitive market has many buyers/sellers and no single participant can influence price — making it objective and unmanipulable. If the market isn't perfectly competitive, the seller could influence the external price and use that to inflate the transfer price.
**Verified:** yes

## Transfer Pricing Methods (Remember)
**Gap:** Only knew variable cost-plus; unaware of absorption cost-plus as a second cost-based variant. Also initially reversed "recovery" — said buyer recovers costs rather than seller.
**Teaching:** Cost-based has two forms: variable cost-plus (only variable costs + margin) and absorption cost-plus (full unit cost including all allocated fixed costs + margin). Absorption favors the seller more because the base is larger. Recovery = the seller getting paid back for production costs through the transfer price.
**Verified:** yes

## Transfer Price Formula (Remember)
**Gap:** Could not state the formula unprompted; initially described the endpoints (cost when idle, market price when full) but couldn't unify them, and said "margin" instead of "opportunity cost."
**Teaching:** Transfer Price = Variable Cost + Opportunity Cost. Opportunity cost = $0 when idle capacity; opportunity cost = foregone contribution margin when at full capacity.
**Verified:** yes
